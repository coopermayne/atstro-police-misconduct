## Thomas Hogg - Key Facts
- Case occurred in 1647 in New Haven Colony
- Servant with bad reputation (liar, thief)
- Had inguinal hernia - caused indecent exposure complaints
- Women including slavewoman Lucretia reported his "filthy nakedness"
- Mrs. Lamberton reported piglets resembled him - "fair and white skin and head"
- Governor Theophilus Eaton conducted "scratch test" on sow
- Hogg refused to confess, saving his life due to the "two witness rule"
- Convicted of lying/stealing, whipped, imprisoned on "mean diet and hard labor"
- Appeared in court 1648 for missing guard duty

## George Spencer - Key Facts
- Executed April 8, 1642 - second execution in CT history
- Described as ugly, balding, with glass eye
- Previously flogged in Boston for receiving stolen goods
- "Habitual troublemaker" - openly faithless, never prayed
- One-eyed sow gave birth to one-eyed piglet
- Charged with bestiality AND "prophane, atheistical carriage"
- Told "he that confesseth and forsaketh his sins shall finde mercie" (ambiguous)
- Had seen child molester only whipped after confession - thought confession = lighter sentence
- Confessed, recanted, confessed again multiple times
- Court counted retracted confession + piglet as two witnesses
- Sow executed by sword, Spencer hanged
- Posthumously pardoned 2015 by Judge John C. Blue
- Called "first verifiable false confession in American history"

## Legal Context
- New Haven Colony rejected English common law
- Used Biblical law derived from Scripture
- Two-witness rule from Deuteronomy 17:6
- Required either 2 witnesses OR confession for capital punishment

## Article Angle
- Lead with two-witness rule as early due process protection
- Contrast Spencer (confessed, executed) with Hogg (refused, survived)
- Connect to modern issues: false confessions, evidentiary standards
