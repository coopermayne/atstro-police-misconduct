## About the LAPD

The Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD) is one of the largest and most influential law enforcement agencies in the United States. Serving the City of Los Angeles, the department has a complex history spanning over 150 years since its founding in 1869.

### Historical Context

Originally established with just six officers, the LAPD has grown into a force of over 9,000 sworn officers serving a city of nearly 4 million residents across 500 square miles. The department has been central to many pivotal moments in American policing history, including:

- **1950s-1960s**: Expansion under Chief William Parker, who professionalized the force but also implemented controversial tactics
- **1965**: Watts Riots, which exposed deep tensions between the LAPD and the Black community
- **1991**: Rodney King beating, which led to the 1992 Los Angeles riots and calls for comprehensive reform
- **2000**: Rampart Scandal, exposing corruption and leading to the federal consent decree
- **2013**: Completion of federal oversight after implementing significant reforms

### Current Leadership

**Chief of Police: Michel Moore** (2018-present)
- 41-year veteran of the LAPD
- Focus on community policing and constitutional policing reforms
- Implementing use-of-force reduction strategies and de-escalation training

**Police Commission**
- Civilian oversight board that sets policy and reviews police conduct
- Current President: Erroll Southers
- Holds weekly public meetings to ensure accountability

### Key Statistics

- **Officers**: Approximately 9,000 sworn personnel
- **Service Area**: 500+ square miles
- **Population Served**: ~4 million residents
- **Budget**: Over $3 billion annually

### Reform Initiatives

Following the 2020 protests and ongoing advocacy, the LAPD has implemented several reform measures:
- Enhanced de-escalation training
- Body-worn camera program expansion
- Mental health crisis response teams (CIRCLE program)
- Updated use-of-force policies emphasizing sanctity of life
- Community Safety Partnership programs in public housing
